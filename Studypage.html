Design content for students

Intro

In 2019, the UX Design team (UXD) at Studyportals did a series evaluation for the core page of Masterportals.com - studypage. The evaluation results showed that the current page design has a few problems: text heavy, patchy interface, lacing visual hierachy...these problems together created a less optimal user experience and is potentially the reason for a low conversion rate. To address these issues, UXD initaited a re-design for the current studypage. This project involved the entire design team, a few product managers and srum teams.

Context & problem

As part of the redesign project, I was responsible for redesigning a specific section on the study page - the "Admission requirements" tab. This tab contains information about requirements a student needs to meet to be accepted to this programme. This is how this section currently looks on desktop and mobile device:

I did an evaluation of the page content which helped identify the following issues:

  • Using tabs for different types of tests makes information less straight-forward;
  • The graph used for displaying scores looks confusing (what is the meaning of the red colored area exactly?) and even breaking in certain scenarios
  • There's no visual hierarchy - so much content! Where should I look?
  • CTA gets buried in all the content thus doesn't stand out
  • Visually not very appealing

When the problems get framed, the design solutions seem to appear by themselves, starting from answer these questions:

  • How can we make information clear and straight-forward for our users?
  • How to introduce a clear hierarchy to contents and actions, thus better inform and guide users?
  • How to provide good user experience for both desktop and mobile?
  • How can we make it more visually appealing?

Process

Understand the content

With these questions in mind, I started a research on the current content. These contents were inserted by data team or universities via an internal content management system, it contains:

  • English level requirement (for international students mainly, usually proven with scores from a well-know test such as IELTS or TOEFL)
  • Academic requirement (can be proven with a GPA score, however for certain disciplines such as business-related programmes, GMAT or GRE might also be required)
  • Other requirement (work experience, technology requirements, and other general requirements)

Cool! That sounds not too difficult, does it? However, further investigation starts revealing the complexity:

  • We have 4 different types of English tests, some we have partnership with, some we don't.
  • We have 3 different types of academic tests, some we have partnership with, some we don't.
  • English tests are alternatives to each other, the other tests aren't.
  • GPA has 2 scores specified (required vs recommended), GRE has 3 scores specified per subject, either required or recommended, GMAT doesn't have a recommended score, however an "average" score.
  • Some tests have additional information specified, some don't.
  • Some tests specified if they are required, some don't.
  • ...

Define the content struture

While writing down all my findings, and looking at the data & specific partnership requirements I received from product team, I started to feel this story might be more complex than it looks....however I believe with the right approach, you will eventually find a solution.

After looking into the data, I remapped the structure of the content based on its priority. Also, I decided to get rid of the tabs and display all the test information directly in a more structured way. The confusing graphs were replaced with more structured score modules.

The score modules are the most important elements in the new design. They should be flexible enough to deal with all different score and partnership scenarios.
